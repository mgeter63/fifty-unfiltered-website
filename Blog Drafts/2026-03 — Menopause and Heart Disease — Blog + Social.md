## Full Blog Post

### What Your Heart Is Trying to Tell You About Menopause (Black Women Need to Hear This)

There is a study that came out in March 2026 that I have not been able to stop thinking about. And I need to talk to you about it directly, because this one is not abstract. This one is about your heart. Literally.

Researchers published findings in JAMA Cardiology showing that women who go through natural menopause before age 40 face a 40 percent higher lifetime risk of developing coronary heart disease. That is a serious number on its own. But here is the part that makes it specifically our conversation.

Black women experience premature menopause at a rate of 15.5 percent. White women experience it at 4.8 percent.

That is more than three times the rate. For the same condition. With the same heart consequences attached.

This is not a coincidence. And it is not biology. Researchers say the disparity likely reflects structural inequities, sustained stress, neighborhood resource gaps, and limited access to quality healthcare throughout our lifetimes. Our bodies are absorbing the cost of a system that has never adequately supported us. And the bill is showing up in our hearts.

**What Premature Menopause Actually Means**

Premature menopause means your ovaries stopped functioning before you turned 40. Early menopause means it happened before 45. These are not rare edge cases. Among Black women, roughly 1 in 7 experiences menopause before age 40.

The reason this matters for heart health is that estrogen plays a protective role in cardiovascular function. When estrogen drops significantly and stays low for more years of your life, the body's ability to manage cholesterol, blood pressure, and inflammation is affected over a longer timeline.

The Northwestern Medicine study that produced these findings followed more than 10,000 U.S. women. Even after the researchers controlled for smoking, obesity, hypertension, and diabetes, premature menopause still carried a 41 percent higher risk of coronary heart disease for Black women. The risk was real on its own, separate from every other factor.

And now the American Heart Association has warned that 60 percent of U.S. women will have cardiovascular disease by 2050.

So this is not a small conversation.

**Why Your Doctor May Not Be Connecting These Dots**

We have already talked on this blog about how doctors are getting menopause wrong for Black women. The dismissal, the undertreating, the tendency to attribute symptoms to stress or weight rather than hormonal shifts.

But this is a different layer of the problem. Even doctors who are somewhat informed about menopause symptoms are often not asking women about the age at which menopause began. The new research specifically recommends that doctors start routinely asking this question and using the menopausal transition as a window to identify women at higher cardiovascular risk and intervene earlier.

In practice, that means the burden is falling on you to bring this information into the appointment yourself. And that is not fair. But it is the reality right now.

**Questions Worth Asking Your Doctor**

If you experienced menopause before 45, this conversation belongs in your next appointment.

Ask your doctor directly: given my age at menopause, what does my cardiovascular risk profile look like? Ask whether your cholesterol, blood pressure, and inflammatory markers have been assessed within the past year. Ask whether hormone therapy was ever discussed with you as an option and whether it is appropriate given your full health picture.

If your doctor dismisses the question or does not know the research I am describing here, that is information too. The UCL study published this same month found that 88 percent of Black women received no menopause education at school and over half felt completely uninformed before the transition began. That gap in information is not your fault. But closing it is now partly your responsibility, because the system has not done its job.

**What You Can Do Beyond the Doctor's Office**

Heart health is not only built in medical appointments. The everyday work matters too, and some of it is genuinely within your control even when the structural pieces are not.

Sleep is a cardiovascular issue. Not just a wellness preference. Poor sleep elevates blood pressure, increases cortisol, and accelerates the kind of inflammation that makes heart disease more likely. If menopause is interrupting your sleep, that is a medical issue worth addressing, not something to power through.

Stress is a physical force on your heart. Chronic stress, the kind that comes from years of overwork, discrimination, caregiving without support, and financial strain, does measurable damage to cardiovascular tissue. Managing stress is not a luxury. For Black women whose bodies have been absorbing these stressors for decades, it is cardiac medicine.

Movement that you actually enjoy is more sustainable than any workout you force yourself through. Walking, dancing, water aerobics, whatever it is, the goal is consistent blood flow over time.

And if you smoke, this is the moment to get very serious about quitting. Smoking and premature menopause together compound cardiovascular risk in ways that stack fast.

**The Bigger Picture**

I want to name something that does not get said enough in health conversations aimed at Black women. The fact that we experience premature menopause at three times the rate of white women is not a personal failing. It is the body's response to a lifetime of navigating systems that were built without us in mind.

Sustained stress, housing instability, environmental factors, years of physically taxing work with inadequate wages and healthcare, all of it accumulates in the body. It accelerates biological processes that would have happened anyway, but earlier and at greater cost.

Your heart has been working hard for you. For all the years you held things together and kept going when you probably should have stopped. It deserves the same kind of attention you have given to everyone else.

So make the appointment. Have the conversation. Bring the research if you need to. You know your body. And now you have the data to back up what it has been trying to tell you.

What has your experience been with doctors and heart health conversations? Have you ever connected menopause to cardiovascular risk before reading this? Tell me in the comments.
