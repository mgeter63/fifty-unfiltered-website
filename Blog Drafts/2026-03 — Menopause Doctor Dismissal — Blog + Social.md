--- BLOG POST ---

Your doctor told you it was normal. You walked out of that office feeling like you imagined the whole thing.

You did not imagine it.

A major study out of University College London, released this month in March 2026, confirmed what Black women have been saying for years. We are going into one of the biggest physical transitions of our lives with almost no information, almost no support, and providers who too often dismiss what we are telling them about our own bodies. 88% of Black women received zero menopause education before the transition hit. More than half felt completely uninformed as they approached it. And of the women who worked up the courage to bring their symptoms to a doctor, the majority described that appointment as negative. They said their symptoms were not taken seriously. Many said they had to essentially self-diagnose to get any real support at all.

This is not a personal failure. This is a system that was not built with us in mind. And we need to stop accepting it.


What Your Body Is Actually Going Through

Menopause is not just hot flashes. That is the first thing to understand.

Among African American women tracked in menopause research, 94% reported night sweats, 93% experienced hot flashes, and 87% dealt with vaginal dryness. Most of these women said their symptoms lasted nine years or longer. Most said they happened every single day.

Nine years. Daily.

And the majority were navigating all of that without a clear explanation, without proper medical guidance, and without anyone sitting down to walk them through what was actually happening in their bodies.

Perimenopause, the transition that happens before your periods officially stop, can begin in your early 40s. It can take years to complete. During that window, your hormones are shifting in ways that affect your sleep, your mood, your memory, your sex drive, your joints, and your cardiovascular system. Most doctors spend less than four minutes discussing menopause with patients. Black women often get considerably less than that.


Why This Is About Your Heart Too

Here is something your doctor may have left out entirely.

A study published in JAMA Cardiology on March 18, 2026 found that women whose periods end before age 40 face a 40% greater lifetime risk of coronary heart disease. Black women are three times more likely than white women to enter premature menopause. Three times.

The connection between early menopause and heart disease is not reaching Black women in plain language. It is buried in academic journals. It gets skipped in appointments that run too short. But heart disease is already the leading cause of death for Black women. Understanding that menopause timing is part of that picture is not optional information. It is urgent.

If you entered menopause early, or if you are currently in the transition and your doctor has not brought up your heart health in that context, that is a conversation you need to start.


Why Black Women Are Being Failed Specifically

Medical research has used white bodies as the default for a very long time. The foundational studies on menopause were conducted predominantly on white women. When Black women present with different symptom patterns, different hormone timelines, and different health histories rooted in decades of stress and systemic disadvantage, many providers simply do not have the training to respond appropriately.

Black women are also more likely to have their pain tolerance overestimated by medical providers. Our concerns get minimized. Our symptoms get attributed to weight or anxiety rather than investigated. And because 88% of us never received basic education about what menopause even is, most Black women do not know they have the right to push back and ask for more.

You have that right.


What You Can Do Starting Now

You do not have to accept a dismissive appointment as the final word on your health. Here is where to start.

Find a menopause-certified provider. The Menopause Society, formerly known as NAMS, maintains a directory of certified practitioners at menopause.org. Search specifically for providers who list experience with patients of color.

Come to every appointment prepared. Write down every symptom, how often it happens, and how it affects your daily life before you walk in. If your provider starts moving past your concerns, say: "I want to make sure we spend time on this before we move on." That sentence alone changes the dynamic.

Ask directly about hormone therapy. Not every woman is a candidate. But many are, and it has been shown to significantly reduce menopause symptoms and protect bone and heart health when started at the right time. Ask your doctor why it does or does not apply to your situation. A doctor who cannot give you a clear answer is a sign you may need a second opinion.

Talk to other women. Community knowledge is real knowledge. Black women who have found helpful providers, who have learned the language that finally gets them heard, who have navigated this transition and come out the other side knowing more, are a resource. Find them. Ask them who their doctor is.


You Know Your Body

You have been taking care of everyone else's health for decades. You have shown up to every appointment, filled every prescription, managed every follow-up. It is time to bring that same energy to yourself.

The researchers behind the UCL study called for mandatory cultural competency training for healthcare professionals, early menopause education in schools, and programs designed by and for women in Black communities. All of that matters. All of it needs to happen. But you do not have to wait for the system to fix itself to demand better care right now.

What would it feel like to walk into your next appointment knowing you do not have to accept "you'll be fine" as a diagnosis? What would change if you walked in already knowing what questions to ask?

Start there. And if you need to find a new doctor who will actually listen, that is not being difficult. That is taking care of yourself.
