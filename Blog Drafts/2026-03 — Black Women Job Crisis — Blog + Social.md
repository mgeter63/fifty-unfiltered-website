## Full Blog Post

### Black Women Are Losing Jobs at a Rate Nobody Is Talking About Loud Enough

Let me tell you what the numbers say, because somebody has to.

One year ago, Black women's unemployment rate was 5.5 percent. Today it is 7.1 percent. That jump happened in twelve months. While white women's unemployment sits at 3.4 percent. While Latina women's unemployment has stayed relatively stable. Black women, and specifically college-educated Black women, have taken the hardest hit of anyone in the current labor market.

Up to 600,000 Black women have lost jobs since January 2025.

Say that number out loud. Six hundred thousand.

This is not a blip. This is not a temporary fluctuation that will correct itself by summer. What is happening to Black women in the workforce right now is a crisis, and the silence around it is its own kind of harm.

**How Did We Get Here**

The job losses are not random. They followed a specific pattern that started the moment the federal government began its largest round of workforce cuts in recent history.

Here is the part that made me stop and sit with it for a minute. Black women make up about 6 percent of the total U.S. labor force. But in the federal workforce, Black women made up 12 percent of employees. That is double the national average. We built careers there. We showed up, we stayed, we got the degrees and the experience, and we worked our way into stability.

Then the cuts came. And Black women absorbed 33 percent of those federal job losses. Not 12 percent. Thirty-three.

The women who were doing everything right got cut at a rate three times their representation. College-educated Black women. The ones who were told education would protect them. The ones who went back for the master's degree, passed the certifications, put in the years.

That is not an accident. That is a pattern. And it matters that we name it clearly.

**What This Means Beyond the Paycheck**

Losing a job is never just about money, even though the money is a crisis too. When you are a Black woman over 50, a job is tied to health insurance you need for conditions your body is already managing. It is tied to retirement contributions that were already behind where they needed to be. It is tied to a sense of identity that nobody should have to apologize for building.

And now a lot of us are sitting at kitchen tables with laptops open, sending applications into a market that already treats Black women over 40 like we are less than what we actually are.

The mental health cost of this is real. Research from 2026 found that 20 percent of Black women over 50 already reported feeling depressed in the past year, before this round of job losses hit. What those numbers look like now, I do not want to guess.

There is also the retirement piece. Black women ages 55 to 59 already had an average retirement balance of $129,933, compared to $328,217 for white women in the same age range. That gap was already unconscionable. Every month out of work without income and without employer retirement contributions makes it wider.

**What You Can Do Right Now**

I want to be careful here. Because there is a version of this conversation that ends with "pull yourself up" advice that puts all the blame and all the responsibility on the woman who just got laid off through no fault of her own. I am not going there.

But I also know that you are someone who figures things out. So here is what is worth considering in the immediate window.

File for unemployment benefits the same week you lose your job, not two weeks later. The system is slow and the clock starts from when you file.

Get a clear picture of your actual financial situation before you make any major moves. What you have in savings, what your monthly minimum looks like, and how many months of breathing room exist. That number matters more than any job listing right now.

Protect your retirement account. If you have a 401k or TSP from a federal job, do not cash it out, even if someone tells you that is the only option. Roll it over. Cashing out triggers taxes and early withdrawal penalties that will cost you more than you can afford to lose right now.

Look at your professional network with fresh eyes. Not for handouts. For information. Someone in your circle knows someone who is hiring. The research consistently shows that jobs are still filled by relationships more than by applications.

And if you were in the federal government, know that there are legal resources specifically for workers who were wrongfully terminated. The Government Accountability Project and the ACLU have both been active in this space. Look them up.

**The Larger Truth**

The Black women who are losing jobs right now did not fail. The system they were working in failed them. There is a difference, and keeping that difference clear in your own head matters.

You still have the skills. You still have the experience. You still have the credentials and the work history and the knowledge that nobody can take back from you.

What was taken was a specific job, in a specific context, by specific policy decisions that targeted the communities where Black women had built their strongest footholds.

That is grief-worthy. Let it be grief-worthy.

And then, when you are ready, figure out the next move from a place of clarity instead of panic. Because the next move exists. It is not obvious yet. But it exists.

What would you want women in your community to know right now about navigating a job loss? Leave it in the comments. We need each other's wisdom more than we need another application portal right now.
