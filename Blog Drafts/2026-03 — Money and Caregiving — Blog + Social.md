## Full Blog Post

### What Happens to Your Money When You Are Also Holding Everyone Else Up

Let me ask you something. When you sat down and worked out your household budget last time, whose needs were in that spreadsheet?

Your mortgage or rent. Your utilities. Groceries. The car note. Maybe your own healthcare if you are lucky enough to have it covered.

What about the money going to your mother's prescriptions? The extra trips you make to the grocery store for her because she cannot drive? The hours you are not billing or working because you left early to take her to the cardiologist? The co-pays you covered quietly because nobody else was stepping up?

For Black women who are caregiving while also working and trying to build some kind of financial future, there is a number that most of us are not tracking. It is the cost of everything we are absorbing for everyone else. And it is eating the retirement savings we were already behind on building.

**What the Data Actually Shows**

Black family caregivers provide an average of 31 hours of care per week. The national average is 23.7 hours. That is more than seven hours above what the average caregiver provides, every single week.

And 64 percent of Black caregivers are working at the same time. Not part-time. Working, most of them full-time, while also providing the equivalent of a nearly full-time second job in unpaid care.

A Pew Research report published in February 2026 found that more than half of African American caregivers are sandwiched between caring for an older person and a younger person under 18, or caring for more than one older adult simultaneously. More than a fifth of Black adults fall into this category.

57 percent of Black family caregivers experience what researchers call high burden caregiving, meaning they are providing a level of care that regularly interferes with work, health, sleep, and financial stability.

And most of them are doing this with no paid family leave, no formal support system, and no one accounting for the financial cost.

**The Money That Is Not Being Saved**

Here is where the financial reality gets specific.

Black women ages 55 to 59 already have an average retirement balance of $129,933, compared to $328,217 for white women in the same age range. That nearly $200,000 gap is not explained by effort or education. It is the accumulated result of wage gaps, housing costs that eat savings, and years of financial decisions made inside a system that was not designed to support Black women's long-term wealth.

Caregiving makes this worse in two specific ways.

The first is direct cost. Out-of-pocket caregiving expenses for family caregivers average about $7,000 per year. For those in high-burden situations, the number can be significantly higher. That is $7,000 that is not going into a savings account, a retirement account, or an emergency fund.

The second is reduced earning capacity. When you are leaving work early, skipping overtime, turning down projects, or cutting your hours to manage caregiving responsibilities, you are also reducing the income you have available to save. You are reducing the employer retirement contributions you receive. And you may be on track to reduce your Social Security benefit, since Social Security calculates benefits based on your highest 35 earning years.

Every year of reduced income during caregiving shows up in retirement.

**The Invisible Tax Nobody Names**

There is a financial concept I want to name clearly because I do not see it discussed enough in conversations about Black women and money.

The invisible tax.

When you are the one who shows up, who covers the shortfall, who manages the care coordination because nobody else in the family is doing it, you are absorbing a tax that the rest of your family is not paying. Your siblings who live out of state. Your cousins who are unavailable. The male relatives who are not expected to step into care roles the way women are.

That tax is real. It has a dollar amount attached to it. And over five or ten years of caregiving, it can represent tens of thousands of dollars in direct costs and reduced earning.

Most women are carrying this tax without ever naming it or calculating it. And you cannot address a problem you have not put a number on.

**The Hard Conversation That Has to Happen**

I know this is uncomfortable territory. Because for many Black women, the caregiving is not just duty. It is love. It is cultural expectation. It is the right thing to do. And none of that is wrong.

But love does not require financial self-destruction. And the most sustainable version of showing up for your family is one where you are not depleting your own future in the process.

If you have siblings or other family members who could be contributing more, financially or in time, that conversation needs to happen. It is hard. It almost always is. But silence costs you money every single month.

If you are the primary caregiver, you are entitled to name that clearly in family conversations and to ask, directly, for contributions from others who have the capacity. This is not greedy. It is equitable.

If your parent or family member has assets, a home, savings, pension income, those resources should be contributing to the cost of their care before your retirement savings take the hit. A conversation with an elder care attorney or a financial advisor who specializes in caregiving situations can help you understand what is available and what is legal.

**What You Can Track Starting Now**

You cannot fix what you cannot see. So this week, I want you to do one thing.

Write down every dollar you have spent on caregiving in the last three months. Everything. The extra groceries. The co-pays. The gas for the medical appointments. The time off work you took unpaid. Estimate that last one honestly.

Now multiply that by four to get your annual caregiving cost.

Look at that number. Because that is what is not going into your future right now.

You do not have to solve it today. But you have to see it first.

**You Cannot Pour From an Empty Account**

The version of caregiving that protects both you and the people you love is one that keeps you financially solvent. That might mean having hard conversations. It might mean calling in reinforcements. It might mean accepting less-than-perfect care coordination because you cannot be everywhere.

But the version of caregiving that drains your retirement, depletes your savings, and leaves you financially vulnerable in your own old age is not sustainable love. It is sacrifice in a way that eventually has no floor.

You matter in this equation. Your financial future belongs in the spreadsheet too.

Where do you see yourself in this? Are you tracking the financial cost of caregiving? Drop it in the comments. This is a conversation we need to keep having.
