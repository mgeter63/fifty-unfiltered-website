--- BLOG POST ---

You keep telling yourself it is just shedding. Seasonal. Stress. The way you have been wearing your hair. You part it on the other side and try not to look too hard.

But something has changed at the crown of your head, and something in you knows it.

Two-thirds of Black women will notice significant hair thinning by the time they reach 50. Two-thirds. That is not a small number. That is most of us. And a significant portion of those women are dealing with something specific that most of us have never been told about by a doctor, a stylist, or anyone else.

It is called Central Centrifugal Cicatricial Alopecia, CCCA for short, and it is the most common form of hair loss in Black women. It is also one of the most misunderstood. And the longer it goes unaddressed, the more permanent the damage becomes.


What CCCA Actually Is

CCCA is a scarring form of alopecia. That word, scarring, is important. It means that as the condition progresses, it destroys the hair follicles themselves. Once a follicle is permanently scarred, the hair that grew from it is gone. Not thinning. Gone.

The hair loss typically starts at the crown of the scalp, the very top, center of your head, and moves outward in a circle. That is where the name comes from. Centrifugal means spreading from the center.

What makes CCCA especially frustrating is that many women do not notice it until significant loss has already occurred. The earliest signs are not always visible to the eye. Before you see the thinning, many women experience burning, stinging, or itching at the scalp. Sometimes tenderness. Sometimes nothing at all. By the time the thinning becomes obvious in the mirror, the window for early intervention has often already passed.

That is why knowing what to look for, and taking it seriously early, matters so much.


Who Is at Risk

CCCA affects women of African descent almost exclusively. The typical patient is a middle-aged Black woman. It can start as early as a woman's 30s and tends to become more visible and more advanced through the 40s and 50s.

Research has identified several factors associated with higher risk. Traction hairstyles, meaning styles that pull on the scalp over time, have been linked to increased risk. Type 2 diabetes is also positively associated with CCCA in multiple studies. Uterine fibroids and vitamin D deficiency have been flagged as well.

For Black women over 50, the overlap of these risk factors is real. Many of us have worn protective or pulled styles for decades. Many of us are managing prediabetes or type 2 diabetes. Many of us are vitamin D deficient and do not know it. CCCA sits at the intersection of all of it.

This is not about what you did wrong. This is about your body and what it needs.


The Emotional Weight of Losing Your Hair

Hair is not a vanity issue for Black women. That framing is wrong and it is dismissive. Hair is identity. It is history. It is culture. It is the way we show up in the world and the way we have been policed for showing up in it. For Black women, hair carries weight that most dermatology textbooks have never adequately addressed.

Losing hair at the crown, the part that is visible when someone looks down at you, or when you glance at yourself in a car window, can be quietly devastating. Many women describe feeling like they are losing themselves. Like they are aging in a way they were not prepared for. Like they cannot talk about it because it sounds like they are being vain when really they are grieving.

You are not being vain. What you are feeling is real. And you deserve real information and real medical support.


What to Do Right Now

If you have noticed thinning at the crown, itching or burning at your scalp, or a change in how your part looks, do not wait.

See a dermatologist who specializes in hair loss in women of color or in skin of color dermatology. Not every dermatologist has training in CCCA. You need someone who does. The Skin of Color Society at skinofcolorsociety.org has a directory of dermatologists with that expertise. Use it.

Get there sooner rather than later. Treatment for CCCA can slow or stop the progression. It may not be able to reverse what has already happened. But it can protect the follicles that are still intact. That is the window you want to move while you have it.

Ask about all contributing factors. Ask your dermatologist to evaluate your vitamin D levels, blood sugar, and scalp health comprehensively. Ask what hairstyle adjustments might reduce ongoing tension or inflammation. Come in ready to have the full conversation, not just the surface one.

Do not let anyone tell you this is just part of getting older. Significant hair loss is not a guarantee. It is a medical condition with treatment options. You have agency here.


One More Thing

Almost half of African American women have experienced hair loss. Nearly half. And yet most of us have never had a doctor explain what CCCA is, how it progresses, or what we can do about it. We find out from other women, from YouTube, from suddenly comparing our hairlines in the mirror and wondering when it changed.

You should not have to find out this way. But now that you know, you can do something about it.

Book the dermatology appointment. Ask the specific questions. And stop waiting to see if it gets worse on its own.
