--- BLOG POST ---

You have the idea. You have the work ethic. You have the drive that comes from knowing nobody is going to hand you anything and building something anyway.

And you are still not building wealth at the rate you deserve.

Black women are the fastest-growing group of entrepreneurs in the United States. That is not a feel-good statistic being tossed around to inspire you. It is documented. Black women are starting businesses at higher rates than any other demographic group in this country. We are creative, resourceful, persistent, and building.

And yet. The wealth gap between Black women entrepreneurs and almost every other group remains enormous. We start more. We scale less. And the reasons have almost nothing to do with how hard we work.


The Starting-to-Scaling Gap

Entrepreneurship research has been tracking a consistent pattern for years. Black women start businesses at impressive rates, but fewer scale beyond the solo-operator stage compared to their white or male counterparts with comparable education and effort.

The gap does not come from lack of ambition or skill. It comes from access. Specifically, access to three things that most Black women entrepreneurs are running without: capital, networks, and proximity to power.

Black women entrepreneurs receive a fraction of the venture capital and small business lending that goes to white founders. Studies show that Black entrepreneurs are denied business loans at significantly higher rates than white business owners with similar credit profiles. The application process assumes a baseline of generational wealth and established credit history that most Black families were systematically prevented from building.

Networks are the second piece. The warm introductions, the mentor relationships, the rooms where decisions get made quietly before they are announced publicly. These networks have historically excluded Black women. You can be the most talented person at the table and if you were never invited to sit down, the talent does not move the needle the same way.

And proximity to power. The people who make funding decisions, who give referrals, who recommend vendors and investors to one another, overwhelmingly look different from us. That is not paranoia. That is a documented pattern across industries.


The Money Under the Business

Here is the part that gets complicated when we talk about entrepreneurship and Black women. The business income question and the personal wealth question are not the same thing.

Many Black women entrepreneurs are generating revenue. Real revenue. Keeping clients, serving customers, growing their platforms. But the income from that work is often replacing a salary that was already low due to the wage gap. Black women earn 66 cents for every dollar paid to white non-Hispanic men. For many Black women, starting a business was not a move toward abundance, it was a way out of a system that was already undervaluing them. The business is better than the alternative. But the starting line was already far behind.

On top of that, the personal wealth picture for Black women is stark regardless of employment. The average Black woman holds just $11,200 in retirement assets. 50% of women ages 55 to 66 have no retirement savings at all. The business may be doing fine while the woman running it has nothing saved for the future, because the revenue went to cover the gap that the wage gap and the wealth gap created over decades.

This is not a business problem. This is a systemic accumulation problem that entrepreneurship alone cannot solve.


What Is Changing in 2026

There are real shifts happening, even if they are not moving as fast as they need to.

The Saver's Match program begins in 2027. It offers a 50% match on retirement contributions up to $2,000 annually for lower and moderate income earners in 401(k) plans. For Black women who are self-employed or running small businesses that do not offer retirement benefits, a solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA combined with this new match could be genuinely significant. This is one of the few policy changes in recent years that research says could specifically help Black women close the retirement savings gap.

More Black women are also deliberately building the networks that were previously closed to them. The creator economy, podcasting, digital platforms, online communities, has lowered the barrier to building an audience and monetizing expertise without needing to be let into rooms that historically kept Black women out. It is not a perfect solution but it is a real one. Several Black women entrepreneurs have built seven-figure businesses entirely through direct community relationship.

Business funding for Black women is also being targeted more specifically. Organizations like the National Black Business Council, Black Girl Ventures, and SheaMoisture's community commerce programs are building infrastructure specifically designed to get capital and resources to Black women entrepreneurs. If you are not aware of what is available, you are leaving real money on the table.


What You Can Do Right Now

If you are a Black woman over 50 who is building a business or thinking about it, here is what matters most.

Treat your business and your retirement as two separate tracks that both need attention. The business paying your bills today does not automatically mean your future is covered. Open a solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA if you are self-employed and contribute to it every single month, even small amounts. Time in market matters even at 55.

Get specific about funding. Research every grant, loan, and funding program specifically designated for Black women business owners before you approach any general funding source. The terms and barrier to entry are different and the rates of approval are significantly better. SCORE, the Small Business Administration, and Black Girl Ventures all have resources worth knowing.

Build a network with intention. Find the rooms, online and in person, where Black women entrepreneurs are supporting each other. Business relationships that come from those communities operate differently than cold outreach into spaces that were not built for you.

Price your work at what it is worth. Not at what you think people will pay. Not at what you charged when you first started and were still figuring it out. At what it is actually worth. The research on the wealth gap for Black women shows that underpricing is one of the most consistent revenue drains across Black women-owned businesses.

Stop building alone. The isolation of entrepreneurship, particularly for Black women who did not grow up around entrepreneurs and do not have a built-in community of peers doing similar work, is real. Find your people. Peer accountability groups for Black women business owners are one of the most consistently cited factors in entrepreneurs who scale.


The Truth About This

Working hard is not enough. Starting a business is not enough. Even doing everything right is not always enough when the systems around you were built to work against you.

That is not a reason to stop. That is a reason to be strategic, specific, and connected in ways that general entrepreneurship advice was never designed to help you be.

You deserve to build something that actually builds you back.

What would it mean for your business to not just support your life today but to actually fund your future? And what is one step you could take this week toward making that true?
