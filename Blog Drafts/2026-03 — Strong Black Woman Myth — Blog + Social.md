--- BLOG POST ---

Somewhere along the way, strong became a cage.

You did not notice it happening. You were just handling things. Showing up. Getting through. Keeping it together when everything and everyone needed you to. And people noticed. They said it with admiration. You are so strong. You can handle anything. I do not know how you do it.

You smiled. You kept going.

But nobody asked if you were okay. And you never said you were not.

This is the Strong Black Woman framework. It is not a compliment, even when it sounds like one. It is an expectation that was placed on Black women long before you were born, that says your worth is tied to your ability to endure, to function, to produce, to never fall apart. And research is now clear that this framework is not protecting Black women. It is making us sick.


The Numbers Behind the Silence

An estimated 27% of Black women have experienced depression at some point in their lives. Nearly three in ten. That is not a small number.

But Black women are only half as likely to seek mental health care as white women. Half. And Black adults overall are 36% less likely to have received mental health treatment in the past year compared to the general population.

The gap between the number of Black women experiencing depression and the number actually getting treatment is enormous. And the Strong Black Woman framework is a significant part of why that gap exists.

When your identity is built around being the one who holds it together, asking for help feels like a betrayal of who you are. Saying "I am not okay" feels like admitting weakness in a world that has always demanded that Black women be strong enough to absorb everything without breaking. The result is millions of women carrying depression, anxiety, grief, and trauma completely alone.

New research published by the American Psychological Association in 2026 is beginning to center what actually helps Black women's mental health, not what the system has historically offered, but what the research shows genuinely works. That shift matters. But it does not help you if you never walk through the door.


Where the Myth Came From

The Strong Black Woman is not something Black women invented for themselves. It is a survival adaptation that was built under conditions that required it.

Black women in this country have navigated centuries of having no right to be vulnerable. No space to be protected. No option to fall apart. The work had to be done. The children had to be raised. The family had to be held together with whatever resources were available. Strength was not a choice for generations of Black women. It was the only option.

What got passed down through family lines and cultural expectations is the message that being strong is who you are. That needing something is a problem. That crying is weakness. That therapy is for people who cannot handle their own lives.

That message kept Black women alive through conditions that should have broken everyone. And it is still killing us slowly now.


What It Actually Looks Like in Real Life

The Strong Black Woman framework shows up in specific ways.

It looks like working through grief without taking any time to actually grieve, because other people still need you.

It looks like not telling your doctor you are struggling because you do not want to seem like you are not managing.

It looks like canceling the therapy appointment you finally made because something came up for someone else.

It looks like being the person everybody unloads on and having nobody to unload on yourself.

It looks like lying awake at 3am with your chest tight and your thoughts racing and telling yourself it is fine, you will get through it, you always do.

You always have gotten through it. That part is true. But getting through is not the same as being well. Surviving is not the same as living.


What Help Actually Looks Like Now

Therapy is not the only form of mental health support, but it is one of the most effective, and Black women deserve access to it without stigma.

If you have never been to therapy or if you tried it and it did not feel like a fit, consider that the right therapist makes all the difference. Specifically, consider looking for a Black therapist. Therapy for Black Girls at therapyforblackgirls.com has a directory of culturally competent therapists. Psychology Today's therapist finder allows you to filter by race and cultural background.

Beyond formal therapy, research on Black women's mental health points to community, specifically, connection with other Black women, as genuinely protective for mental health. Not just social contact. Emotional closeness. The kind where you can say what is actually true and be heard by someone who understands the context without having to explain it.

That kind of community is not automatic. For many Black women over 50, it has to be built deliberately. The friendships from earlier stages of life may have drifted. The children have grown and the social structures that came with raising them are gone. Building genuine connection at this stage takes intention.

It is worth the effort.


Putting the Myth Down

You do not have to stop being strong. You can be strong and be in therapy at the same time. You can be strong and cry in your car on the way home. You can be strong and tell the truth when someone asks how you are.

Strength is not the problem. The problem is the version of strength that requires you to disappear your own needs completely.

You were not put on this earth to run on empty for other people indefinitely. You were not built to absorb everyone else's weight and never set any of it down. The fact that you have done it this long is a testament to you. But the fact that you have done it this long without real support is something we should all be angry about.

What would it feel like to let someone help you? Not to manage everything alone, just for a week, just in one area, but to actually let someone in?

That is not weakness. That is what the rest of us have been getting to do all along.
