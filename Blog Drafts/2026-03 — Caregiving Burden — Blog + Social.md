--- BLOG POST ---

You called your mother this morning before 8am because she needed help with her medication. You texted your adult son about his electricity bill. You reminded your sister about her doctor's appointment. You have not eaten a real breakfast in two weeks because by the time you think of yourself, the day is already halfway gone.

Nobody asked how you were doing.

Nobody ever does.

This is the life that millions of Black women over 50 are living, and it has a name even if most people are too uncomfortable to say it out loud. It is called caregiver burnout. And for Black women specifically, the data shows that we are carrying more of this weight, with fewer resources and less support, than almost any other group in this country.

18% of Black women provide unpaid caregiving for a family member compared to 12% of white women. 57% of Black family caregivers are doing what researchers classify as high burden caregiving, meaning they are providing an average of 30 hours of care every single week. On top of their jobs. On top of their own health. On top of their own lives.

And almost none of them are doing it with any formal help.


The Sandwich Generation Nobody Is Talking About

The phrase "sandwich generation" usually conjures an image of a stressed suburban woman managing toddlers and aging parents at the same time. But the reality for Black women over 50 looks different.

Many of us are not raising young children. We are supporting adult children who are still financially vulnerable, managing a parent or two who needs daily help, and often caring for a grandchild while we do it. We are caring for more than one generation. Nearly a third of Black caregivers are providing care across multiple generations simultaneously.

And 64% of Black family caregivers are employed while doing it. Working full-time or close to it, then coming home to 30 hours a week of unpaid caregiving on top of that. Most without paid family leave. Most without any formal support system coordinating the care alongside them.

56% of Black caregivers report significant difficulty coordinating with healthcare professionals for the people they are looking after. So we are not just doing the physical and emotional work of caregiving. We are also navigating a system that does not make it easy, often alone.


What It Does to Your Body

This is not just exhausting. It is dangerous.

The physical toll of long-term high-burden caregiving on the caregiver is well-documented. Elevated cortisol from chronic stress contributes to high blood pressure, disrupted sleep, weakened immune function, and increased risk of cardiovascular disease. Black women are already at higher risk for all of those conditions before caregiving is added to the picture.

When you do not sleep, when you do not eat well, when you skip your own medical appointments because you are taking your mother to hers, when you run on empty for months and then years, your body keeps that score. It does not forget. And it will eventually present the bill.

Many Black women find out they are seriously ill at exactly the moment they finally have a few minutes to breathe. That is not a coincidence.


Why We Cannot Just Say No

There are a thousand think pieces about self-care and setting limits with family. Most of them were not written with Black women in mind.

The expectation that Black women will be the family caretaker is not just a personal preference. It is cultural, historical, economic, and deeply embedded. Black families have survived by relying on each other when systems failed them. Black women specifically have been the connective tissue of those families for generations. The expectation that we will show up does not go away when we turn 50. If anything, it intensifies.

You cannot just decide to stop caring about your mother because you read an article about limits. That is not how love works. That is not how family works. And that is not how cultural expectation works.

But you can start making room for yourself inside that caregiving reality. Not instead of it. Inside of it.


What You Can Actually Do

You do not have to choose between caring for everyone and caring for yourself. But you do have to stop treating yourself as the last item on the list.

Ask for help from every direction available. If there are siblings, partners, adult children, church members, neighbors who could share even one task per week, ask. People do not offer because they assume you have it handled. They need to be asked directly.

Explore what services exist. Area Agencies on Aging provide local resources for caregivers and the people they care for. Many offer respite care, meaning temporary relief care so you can rest. eldercare.acl.gov connects you to local services. Most people do not know these exist.

Make one non-negotiable appointment for yourself each month. Not a luxury. A medical appointment, a therapy session, a haircut that you actually keep. One commitment to yourself that does not get canceled when someone else needs something.

Tell your doctor you are a caregiver. Caregiving is a significant health risk factor. It belongs in your medical history. Your doctor should know it so they can monitor your health with that context.

Find community. Other Black women who are in the middle of this will understand in a way that most people around you may not. That understanding itself, just being seen in what you are carrying, matters more than you might realize.


One Last Thing

You became the person everyone calls because you are capable and you love them and you would not change that. But being capable does not mean you are not tired. Being the strong one does not mean you do not need help. And being available for everyone else does not mean your own health and life can run on fumes indefinitely.

Who is carrying you?

That is not a rhetorical question. Think about it. Write the answer down. And if the honest answer is nobody, that is the thing that needs to change first.

What would it look like for someone to genuinely show up for you right now? And what would it take to let them?
